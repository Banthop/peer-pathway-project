## Chapter 1: What a Spring Week Actually Is (And What They Are Really Assessing)

### The reality nobody tells you upfront

A spring week, insight programme, or first-year diversity programme is, at its core, a 3 to 5 day recruiting event. The bank or firm is spending somewhere between £500 and £2,000 per person to run it. They are not doing that out of goodwill. They are doing it because it is the most cost-effective way to identify talent early and lock it in before competitors can get to it.

The conversion pathway looks like this: spring week leads to summer internship application fast-tracking or direct invite, summer internship leads to graduate scheme offer. By getting you into the spring week, they are betting that you will return and eventually join full-time. Some firms convert spring weekers to summer interns at rates above 70%. They are highly motivated to convert the people they like.

This changes how you should think about the week. You are not there to "get a feel for the industry." You are there to prove that you should come back. Everything you do during the week should be filtered through that lens.

### What they are actually assessing

Banks and firms assess four things during spring weeks, roughly in this order:

**1. Cultural fit (can we see ourselves working with this person every day?)**
This is assessed by everyone you interact with: analysts, associates, HR, even the reception staff at some firms. It is not about being liked. It is about being easy to work with, professionally confident, and aware of the environment you are in. Banks lose a lot of money on bad hires. They are looking for red flags as much as green flags.

**2. Intellectual curiosity (are you actually interested, or are you CV-building?)**
The dead giveaway for a CV-builder is someone who asks generic questions about "the typical day" or "what you enjoy about the role." The person who has clearly read about the division, references a recent deal or market event, and asks something specific immediately signals that they are genuinely engaged. You do not need to know everything. You need to show that you tried.

**3. Communication and composure (can you hold yourself together under mild pressure?)**
The case study, the presentation, the panel Q&A, the networking event, the moment an MD asks you something you do not know the answer to. All of these are pressure tests. The ones who convert are not the ones with the best answers. They are the ones who stay calm, structure their thinking out loud, and do not crumble or bluster.

**4. Reliability and work ethic (would you stay until midnight if needed?)**
Junior banking and finance roles are demanding. They are not assessing whether you will be happy about that. They are assessing whether you will do it without complaint and without making it everyone else's problem. Turning up on time, being prepared, offering to help, and not being the person who leaves first all signal the right work ethic.

### The hidden assessment: how you treat junior staff

This is the thing nobody tells you. Every graduate analyst and associate you interact with is an informal assessor. HR will ask them directly: "Which spring weekers stood out? Which ones would you want on your team?" The person who spent the week schmoozing partners while ignoring the analysts will get quietly marked down. The person who treated the analyst as a real person, showed genuine curiosity about their experience, and sent a thoughtful LinkedIn message after their conversation will get quietly marked up.

Map out who you have spoken to each day. Make sure the list includes analysts and not just senior people.

### The timeline of a typical spring week

Day 1: Induction, firm overview, introductions. Light on real work. Heavy on first impressions. This is when the most assessment happens per hour. Be switched on from the moment you walk in.

Day 2-3: Desk time, shadowing, small tasks. The pace picks up. This is when relationships are built. Have prepared questions for the people you are sitting with. Do the small tasks quickly and accurately.

Day 4: Often the group task or case study. Energy levels are usually lower across the cohort. Being visibly engaged when others are flagging is powerful.

Day 5: Presentations, wrap-up, feedback sessions. This is the last impression. The follow-up process starts the same evening.

## Chapter 2: The First Impression Framework

### You have 90 seconds

Research on first impressions in professional settings is consistent: the other person has formed a view of you before you have finished your first sentence. This is not about being fake or performing. It is about channelling the best version of yourself under pressure, which is a skill you can practice.

The first impression framework has three components: physical presence, verbal opening, and the first five minutes.

### Physical presence

**Dress code:** When in doubt, overdress. Not the most expensive outfit - the most appropriate outfit. For men at IB and consulting firms: a well-fitted suit or smart blazer and trousers, a pressed shirt, clean shoes. For women: a suit, smart dress, or blazer with smart trousers. The specific colour does not matter as much as the fit and cleanliness. Avoid anything that creases badly or will look dishevelled by 3pm.

The rule is: look like the person at the firm who dresses slightly better than the average analyst, not like a student who borrowed someone's suit.

**Body language before you speak:** Posture matters when you are sitting in presentations and inductions. Sit forward in your chair. Keep your phone face-down or not on the desk. Avoid crossing your arms. Make eye contact when someone is speaking to you. These are small things, but they are continuously noticed.

**The handshake:** Still relevant at IB firms and boutiques. Firm, not crushing. One or two shakes, then release. If you are anxious and your hands are sweaty (it happens), wipe them subtly before you enter the room.

### The verbal opening

The most common question on Day 1 is some version of "tell me about yourself." Have a 45-second version ready that covers: where you study, what you study, one specific interest or experience related to finance or the industry you are in, and one forward-looking statement about why you are there. Do not read from a script. Say it naturally. Practice it out loud before the day starts.

Bad version: "I'm a second-year at Exeter studying economics. I've always been interested in finance and I'm really excited to be here."

Better version: "I'm in my second year at Exeter, studying economics. I've been following the leveraged buyout market for the past year, especially the activity in the healthcare sector, and I'm here because I wanted to understand how the advisory side of that actually works day-to-day."

The second version is not longer. It is more specific. Specificity signals preparation. Preparation signals that you care.

### The first five minutes of a conversation

The principle here is simple: ask one good question early, and listen properly to the answer. Most spring weekers ask questions and then nervously wait for the answer to finish so they can say something about themselves. The person who actually listens and responds to what was said creates a real conversation, not an interview exchange. That is what "good to talk to" means in practice.

Prepared questions for the first day:

- "What does a typical deal or project look like for your team right now?" (shows interest in actual work)
- "What surprised you most about the role when you first started?" (opens up a real, unscripted conversation)
- "What does the firm look like to someone who's been here for a while - what changes?" (shows long-term thinking)

Avoid: "What do you enjoy about your job?" Too generic. The analyst has answered it 20 times today.

### Small talk: the underrated skill

At networking events, lunches, and end-of-day drinks, the conversation will be less structured. Small talk is not meaningless. It is how you build rapport with people who are deciding whether they like you. Topics that work: asking about their route into the firm, their university experience, how they found the first year, things happening in the market. Topics to avoid: anything controversial, asking how much they earn, complaining about anything, talking negatively about other firms.

One rule for networking events: spend no more than 10 minutes with any one person. Keep circulating. At the end of the event, you want to have had at least 5 genuine conversations, not 2 deep ones.

## Chapter 3: How to Network During a Spring Week (The Coffee Chat System)

### Why most spring week networking fails

The typical spring weeker approaches networking as a transaction: get in front of as many senior people as possible, ask questions, make it memorable. The problem is that everyone else is doing the same thing. The result is that the most networked people at the firm are exhausted by the end of Day 1, and the spring weekers blur into each other.

The coffee chat system works differently. It focuses on depth over breadth with junior staff, it creates a repeatable structure for each conversation, and it generates a paper trail (in the form of LinkedIn messages) that keeps you visible after the week ends.

### The system

**Step 1: Target junior staff first.**
Aim to have a genuine conversation with at least 2 analysts or associates per day. Not the head of the desk. The person who joined 1-3 years ago. They remember what it felt like to be in your position. They are more willing to be honest. And they are the people HR talks to.

**Step 2: Book the conversation properly.**
Do not ambush people at their desks for 30-minute conversations. Send a quick message (email or the internal messaging system) the evening before: "Hi [name], I'm one of the spring weekers on [desk/division]. Would you have 10-15 minutes tomorrow to chat about your experience on the team?" Ten minutes is less threatening than "a coffee chat" and often turns into longer because the conversation flows.

**Step 3: The conversation structure.**
Open with one specific question about their work. Then ask about their path into the role. Then ask what they wish they had known during their own spring week or internship. This third question is the most valuable because it tells you exactly what the firm values and what the actual conversion criteria look like. Close by asking if there is anyone else on the team they would recommend you speak to.

**Step 4: The immediate follow-up.**
Within 2 hours of the conversation, connect on LinkedIn with a personalised message. Reference one specific thing from the conversation. This is how you stay out of the "which one were they again?" pile.

### The network map

On Day 1 evening, draw a rough map of the team or division you are sitting in. Identify: who makes hiring decisions, who you have already met, who you still want to speak to, and who is in your cohort that seems well-connected (sometimes a spring weeker in your cohort has a contact at the firm - find out early).

By Day 3 you should have spoken to: your direct desk supervisor, at least 3 analysts or associates, one HR contact, and if possible one VP or director (though senior people are less important than junior staff for conversion purposes).

### Dealing with nerves

Everyone is nervous. The analysts know you are nervous. They were nervous on their spring week. What they are watching is how you handle the nerves, not whether you have them. If you blank on a question, say: "That's a good question, let me think about that for a second." Then think. Then answer. That is more impressive than a flustered, fast, wrong answer.

## Chapter 4: Standing Out Without Being Annoying (The Signal vs Noise Principle)

### The problem with "standing out"

Every piece of advice about spring weeks tells you to stand out. The result is a cohort of 20 people all trying to be the most memorable person in the room simultaneously. This creates noise. It becomes exhausting for the staff, and the people who are loudest are not always the people who get return offers.

The signal vs noise principle is this: every action you take either adds useful information about you (signal) or adds clutter that makes you harder to assess (noise). The goal is maximum signal, minimum noise.

### What counts as signal

- Asking a specific, well-researched question in a skills session that shows you have done reading beforehand
- Completing a task slightly ahead of the deadline with a note explaining your approach
- Offering to stay late on a day when others are leaving, even for 30 minutes
- Sending a brief end-of-day update to your desk supervisor without being asked
- Noticing something in the team's work that relates to something you studied or read about
- Being the person who makes the group presentation slightly better by staying for an extra hour on the draft

### What counts as noise

- Asking lots of questions in group settings to appear engaged but not listening to the answers
- Dominating group tasks and presenting every section of the work
- Mentioning other prestigious programmes or firms you have been accepted to ("I was also at Goldman last week...") - this is common and almost always backfires
- Arriving very early and staying very late every day to signal work ethic, but not actually producing better work
- Sending long, elaborate thank you emails to everyone on Day 1 - feels like a script

### The consistent performer advantage

Spring week conversion is not about peaks. It is about the absence of troughs. The person who has two great days and two average days is less likely to convert than the person who is solid and professional every single day. Banks specifically look for consistency because they need people who perform on the day the client is calling, not just on the day they feel like it.

Practically, this means: no days where you coast. Even if the task is boring, do it properly. Even if the networking event is at the end of an exhausting day, show up present and engaged for the first hour.

### The one big move

Every spring week has one moment where you can make a meaningful impression that goes beyond "good candidate." It might be asking an insightful question that stops an MD mid-sentence. It might be a presentation slide that is noticeably better than everyone else's. It might be a conversation with a senior person where you say something that genuinely surprises them.

You cannot manufacture this moment. But you can create the conditions for it to happen by being prepared, being present, and not playing it safe all week.

## Chapter 5: The Follow-Up System

### Why follow-up is where most spring weekers fail

The spring week ends and 80% of participants do nothing. They had a good time, maybe connected on LinkedIn, and assume the firm will be in touch. The 20% who get return offers are doing something the other 80% are not: they are staying visible.

Follow-up is not about being pushy. It is about giving the people who liked you a reason to remember your name when the HR team is deciding who to fast-track.

### The 48-hour rule

In the 48 hours after the spring week ends, do three things:

**1. Send a thank you email to HR.**
One email, addressed to the graduate recruitment contact you have been in touch with. Two paragraphs. First: specific things you learned or valued during the week. Second: your genuine interest in the summer programme and a brief statement of why you want to return. Do not make it long. Long thank you emails are rarely read in full.

**2. Send LinkedIn connection requests with personalised notes to every person you had a real conversation with.**
This is non-negotiable. The window is 48 hours because after that, the conversation has faded and the personalised message feels less natural. Each note should reference one specific thing from your conversation. Nothing generic.

**3. Send one "follow-up insight" message to the analyst or associate you got on best with.**
This is the move most people do not make. It looks like this: "Hi [name], I've been thinking about what you said about [specific thing]. I came across [article/deal/data point] that made me think of that conversation. Wanted to share it." This is not about impressing them with your knowledge. It is about showing that you were genuinely listening and that the conversation stayed with you. That is memorable.

### The two-week follow-up

Two weeks after the spring week, if you have not heard anything about next steps, send one short follow-up email to the HR contact. One sentence asking about the timeline for summer programme decisions and reiterating your interest. That is it. Do not apologise for following up. Do not send three emails.

A note on tone: follow-up emails fail when they sound either desperate or entitled. The tone to aim for is confident and professional, like someone who is interested but also has other options. Even if you feel desperate about a specific offer, do not let that come through in writing. The recruiter who receives a calm, well-written follow-up will respond more positively than to one that reads as anxious.

### Building a LinkedIn profile that works

Before you start sending connections, make sure your LinkedIn profile is in order. The things that matter:

- A professional headshot (does not need to be expensive, just clear and non-distracting background)
- A headline that says more than "student at X university" - something like "Economics student at LSE, interested in investment banking and credit markets"
- A summary section that reads like a person wrote it, not a HR keyword list
- Every relevant experience listed, including society roles, competitions, relevant coursework
- Skills endorsed by actual connections, not just self-added

The spring week itself should go on your LinkedIn the week it ends. Include the firm name, the division, and 2-3 bullet points about what you worked on. This signals to the market (and to the firm, who will look you up) that you are serious.

### The staying-top-of-mind system (months after the spring week)

The people who eventually get summer internship offers are not always the highest performers during the week. Sometimes they are the people who stayed consistently present in the network. Every 4-6 weeks, find a reason to interact with one of the contacts you made:

- React or comment on a LinkedIn post they write
- Send a brief message when a big deal or market event happens that is relevant to their team
- Share an article or piece of research that connects to something you discussed

This is not about being sycophantic. One genuine interaction every 5 weeks is all it takes to stay in the front of someone's mind when they are asked "who from the spring week should we invite to the summer internship assessment centre?"

## Chapter 6: How Return Offers Actually Work

### The decision-making process

Every firm has a slightly different process for converting spring weekers to summer interns, but the structure is roughly consistent:

**Desk feedback:** Your desk supervisor and the analysts you worked with submit written or verbal feedback. This is weighted heavily. If the desk does not rate you, almost nothing else can save you.

**HR evaluation:** Graduate recruitment typically rates spring weekers on: presentation performance, networking engagement, attitude, and cultural fit. This is assessed through their own observations and through informal conversations with staff.

**Peer assessment:** Some firms ask spring weekers to rank each other or provide feedback. Be aware of this. The person in your cohort who you undermined in the group presentation remembers.

**Formal assessment tasks:** Most banks use the case study or group presentation as a formal assessment input. This is scored and fed into the decision.

The final decision is usually made by a small committee consisting of HR and one or two business reps from the relevant division. They review the inputs and make offers. At most firms, this happens within 2-6 weeks of the spring week ending.

### Conversion rates: what the data says

Conversion rates vary enormously by firm. Some firms (Barclays Discovery) fast-track almost all spring weekers to the summer internship assessment centre. Some boutiques (Evercore, Rothschild) have much lower conversion rates because they run smaller cohorts and are more selective at the spring week stage itself.

General patterns:
- Bulge bracket banks typically invite 40-70% of spring weekers to the summer AC
- Elite boutiques typically invite 30-50%
- Not all AC invites lead to offers - the AC itself has its own pass rate
- At some firms, getting a return offer from a spring week skips the AC entirely (fast-track)

The numbers are in the firm-specific chapters. Use them for calibration, not as gospel.

### What triggers a return offer (the real reasons)

**Desk performance:** Did you add value? Did you complete tasks correctly? Were you useful?

**The "would I want this person on my team?" test:** This is the actual question the desk supervisor is answering. It is qualitative. It is emotional. You pass this test by being competent, pleasant to work with, and professional under pressure. Not by being the smartest person in the room.

**Visibility with decision-makers:** Did the right people know your name by the end of the week? This does not mean forcing conversations with directors. It means that when your name comes up in the decision meeting, there is someone in the room who has a positive impression of you.

**No red flags:** The absence of negative incidents is as important as the presence of positive ones. Many candidates fail to convert not because they did anything wrong exactly, but because there was a moment that gave someone pause - a dismissive comment, a moment of visible impatience, a piece of work submitted below standard.

### What to do if you do not get the offer

First: it is not the end. The people who get the return offer are not necessarily better than the people who do not. They were better positioned during that specific week. Circumstances, desk allocation, and cohort dynamics all play a role.

Second: ask for feedback. Email the HR contact 2-3 weeks after the decision. Ask for a call to get developmental feedback. Some firms will give this. Some will not. If they do, listen without becoming defensive. The feedback will often tell you exactly what to fix for the next application.

Third: the network you built is still valuable. The analysts who liked you still like you. Keep those connections warm. People move firms. Referrals happen from unexpected places. A spring week that did not convert can still lead to an offer, just on a different timeline.

## Banking and Trading

### The culture of investment banking

Investment banking is, at its core, a client service business. The hours are long because clients do not keep regular hours. The culture is hierarchical because decisions have real consequences and accountability matters. Spring weekers are entering an environment where the analysts are already working 80+ hours a week, the associates are managing multiple live deals, and everyone has limited time to handhold.

The students who thrive in spring weeks at IB firms are the ones who require minimal supervision. Show up knowing how to use Excel at a basic level. Know the difference between an M&A deal, an ECM transaction, and a leveraged finance deal. Understand what the specific division you are sitting in actually does before you arrive.

### What a typical IB spring week looks like

Most IB spring weeks follow a similar structure:
- Morning: Desk time, shadowing analysts, small tasks (updating models, formatting decks, research)
- Afternoon: Formal sessions (skills training, divisional overviews, Q&A panels)
- Evening: Networking events, sometimes end-of-day team drinks

The tasks assigned to spring weekers are rarely intellectually demanding. You will be asked to do things like research a comparable companies set, format a pitch deck slide, or find data on a specific company. The quality of your work matters, but what matters more is your attitude toward the tasks. Nobody expects you to build a DCF from scratch in week one. They do expect you to do the research task accurately and ask a sensible question if something is unclear.

### What banks look for in spring weekers

**Commercial awareness:** Do you follow markets? Do you have an opinion on a current macro trend, a recent deal, or a sector your desk works in? This does not need to be expert-level. It needs to be genuine.

**Numeracy and analytical thinking:** Not the ability to do complex maths, but the ability to look at a data set and say something coherent about it.

**Presentation and communication:** IB analysts spend a significant part of their time making slides. Can you structure an argument clearly? Can you communicate under pressure?

**Resilience and work ethic:** Will you still be engaged and professional on Day 4 when you are exhausted?

### Sales and Trading vs Investment Banking

If you are doing a S&T placement versus an IBD placement, the assessment criteria shift. S&T values quicker thinking, higher risk tolerance, and the ability to make fast decisions with incomplete information. IBD values careful thinking, accuracy, and the ability to structure complex problems.

In S&T spring weeks: ask about specific products, show interest in market dynamics, be willing to have opinions and defend them. In IBD: show you can work carefully, think through a problem methodically, and communicate clearly.

### Trading and Quant (Jane Street, Citadel, Optiver)

These programmes are fundamentally different from banking spring weeks. The assessment is heavily quantitative from Day 1. Expect probability puzzles, mental arithmetic, logic problems, and market-making simulations. Cultural fit still matters but the bar is set differently: they are looking for genuine intellectual curiosity, comfort with ambiguity, and the ability to think clearly and quickly.

If you are going into a quant trading spring week, practice: quick mental maths, expected value calculations, probability puzzles, and market-making scenarios. The firm-specific chapters for Jane Street and Citadel have more detail.

## Asset Management and Private Equity

### The AM spring week vibe

Asset management spring weeks have a different energy from IB. The pace is slower, the culture is generally more relaxed, and the hours are more predictable. This can be misleading. The assessment is just as rigorous; it is just less obvious.

AM firms care intensely about investment thinking. Every student who goes through a BlackRock or Schroders spring week will at some point be asked to discuss a stock, a sector, or a macro theme. If you have not prepared for this, it will show immediately.

### Preparing an investment idea

Pick one stock. Research it properly: understand the business model, the competitive positioning, the key risks, the valuation (at a high level - P/E relative to peers is fine). Have a view on whether it is attractive or not and be able to defend it in 3-5 sentences.

This is not about being right. It is about showing that you can think like an investor. The person assessing you has heard dozens of generic "I like Apple because everyone uses iPhones" pitches. A well-structured bear case on a company you think is overvalued is more interesting and more impressive.

### What makes someone stand out in AM

The question "what stock would you buy today and why?" is almost always asked. But the deeper conversation that follows it is what decides how you are remembered. The spring weekers who convert are the ones who can have a real back-and-forth about a market topic without either freezing up or over-stating their certainty. Intellectual humility matters: "I think X, but the key risk to that view is Y" is the right structure.

### Private Equity at spring week stage

PE spring weeks are rare. Carlyle and Blackstone run insight programmes but they are extremely selective. The people who get in typically have a strong academic profile and at least some prior finance experience (even if just a school internship or finance society leadership). At PE spring weeks, the focus is on: deal analysis, fundamental business valuation, and the ability to think about companies from an owner's perspective rather than an advisor's perspective.

## Consulting

### Consulting spring weeks: what actually happens

Consulting spring weeks (MBB and Big 4) tend to be heavier on formal programming than finance spring weeks. Expect: firm overview sessions, case study workshops, guided case practice with consultants, and often a team case study competition.

The case study is the centrepiece. Unlike a finance spring week where the assessment is more diffuse, in consulting the case study is where the formal assessment happens. You will be scored on: structure, hypothesis-driven thinking, quantitative reasoning, communication, and teamwork.

### The "consulting thinking" signal

The most important thing to demonstrate in a consulting spring week is structured thinking. This means: when you are asked a question, you do not immediately give an answer. You organise the problem, identify the key components, and then work through them in order.

A simple example: "How would you estimate the market size for electric vehicles in the UK?"

Bad answer: "I think the market is growing quickly so it's probably quite large..."

Good answer: "I'd approach this by estimating the total number of vehicles sold in the UK per year, identifying what percentage are currently EVs, then applying a growth rate based on policy trends and infrastructure. Let me start with the total market..."

The second answer signals structured thinking even before you get to the numbers. That is what consulting firms are looking for.

### The case study competition

Most consulting spring weeks include a group case study that is presented to a panel. The dynamics here are different from individual case interviews. The assessors are watching: who structures the group's approach, who listens to others and builds on their ideas, who communicates the final answer clearly, and who stays calm when the panel asks hard questions.

Do not try to dominate the group exercise. The person who takes over and ignores their teammates' contributions is consistently marked down. The person who helps the group arrive at a better answer than they would have individually is consistently marked up.

## Big 4 and Professional Services

### What makes Big 4 spring weeks different

Big 4 spring weeks (Deloitte, EY, KPMG, PwC) are more structured and less high-pressure than IB or MBB. The conversion rate to summer internships is generally higher. The culture is more collegiate and the assessment is more transparent.

This does not mean they are easy. The Big 4 are highly selective at the spring week stage, and the summer internship pipeline is competitive. The difference is that the assessment criteria are often more clearly communicated and the environments are less hierarchical.

### What Big 4 firms look for

Commercial awareness matters here too, but in a slightly different way. Big 4 firms serve clients across all sectors, so the relevant question is not "what do you think about the equity markets?" but "can you think about business problems from multiple angles?" Industry and sector interest is valued, but breadth of commercial thinking is more important than depth in any one area.

For audit and tax practices: attention to detail, accuracy, and the ability to work through complex information systematically. For advisory and consulting practices: similar to MBB, structured thinking and communication.

### Soft skills carry more weight

At Big 4 firms, the cultural fit and soft skills assessment is weighted more heavily than at most IB firms. Being good to work with, client-facing confidence, and clear communication are assessed throughout. The person who is technically capable but comes across as difficult or arrogant is much more likely to not convert at a Big 4 firm than at an IB firm where pure performance can sometimes override cultural concerns.

## Law and Vacation Schemes

### The vacation scheme as an extended interview

Law vacation schemes typically run for 2-4 weeks and are significantly longer than finance spring weeks. The extended length means the assessment is more gradual and the opportunities to recover from a bad day are greater. However, it also means sustained performance is more important.

Vacation schemers sit in departments, complete real work under supervision, attend firm-wide events, and participate in skills training. The formal assessment usually includes a written exercise, a group exercise, and a presentation.

### What law firms look for

**Commercial awareness:** More so than almost any other profession, law firms want students who understand the commercial context of legal work. A deal is not just a legal document; it is a commercial transaction with real business implications. Understanding why the client is doing the deal, what their priorities are, and what risk looks like from their perspective is what commercial awareness means in a law context.

**Attention to detail:** Legal work involves documents where a single word matters. The student who submits sloppy work is signalling that they do not understand what the job actually requires.

**Communication:** Can you explain a complex legal concept clearly to a non-lawyer? This is a core skill for client-facing solicitors.

### The most common mistake

Treating the vacation scheme like a passive learning experience rather than an active assessment. Students who just attend sessions, do the tasks set, and wait to be impressed by the firm tend not to convert. The ones who convert are the ones who show initiative: asking good questions, offering to help with additional work, showing genuine curiosity about the clients and matters the team is working on.

### Goldman Sachs

**Programme:** Goldman Sachs Spring Insight (various divisions: IBD, S&T, Equity Research, Asset Management)

**Typical dates:** Late March to mid-April, 1-week programmes split by division

**What the programme involves:**

### Jane Street

**Programme:** Jane Street INSIGHT Programme

**Typical dates:** April 13-18, 2026

**What the programme involves:**
Jane Street's INSIGHT programme is unlike any other spring week on this list. It is an intensive week of lectures, problem sets, and trading simulations, with Jane Street's own traders and researchers teaching throughout. There are sessions on probability, estimation, options theory, game theory, and market-making. This is not shadowing. This is learning, doing, and being assessed on your thinking in real time.

Quantitative reasoning, logical thinking, comfort with uncertainty, ability to learn quickly, and intellectual curiosity. Jane Street does not care about your knowledge of financial products when you arrive. They care about your ability to reason through novel problems clearly and quickly.

**Conversion rate:** Approximately 40-50% of INSIGHT participants are invited to the summer internship. The summer internship itself is the gateway to a full-time offer.

**Tips from past attendees:**
- Do not bluff. If you do not know something, say so and reason through it from first principles. Jane Street respects honest, careful thinking over confident wrong answers.
- Practice: probability puzzles, expected value calculations, mental arithmetic, and market-making scenarios. These will come up.
- The market-making simulations require you to make quick decisions with incomplete information. The key is not to get the "right" answer but to demonstrate a clear, consistent reasoning process.
- Every conversation with a Jane Street employee is a continuation of the assessment. They are genuinely curious people who will engage you in real intellectual discussions. Lean into it.

**Insider tip:** Jane Street does not care about your university at all. They care entirely about your thinking. A student from a non-target university with genuine quantitative ability converts here at the same rate as an Oxford student who cannot reason under pressure.

## The 48-Hour Rule: A Summary

Everything in this handbook can be distilled into one rule, and it applies to every touchpoint of your spring week journey:

**Act within 48 hours, or do not act at all.**

After a spring week conversation: connect on LinkedIn within 48 hours.
After the programme ends: send your thank you email within 48 hours.
After a meaningful article or market development: send your follow-up insight within 48 hours.
After any feedback you receive: act on it within 48 hours.

The 48-hour window is when the memory is fresh, the connection is warm, and the action feels natural. After that window closes, the action becomes awkward, then eventually impossible.

The students who get return offers are rarely the most talented students in the cohort. They are the students who moved the fastest on the things that matter.

## Bonus Section: Managing Your Mindset During the Week

Nobody talks about this enough, and it is one of the things that most directly affects performance.

### The anxiety trap

The spring week environment is designed, intentionally or not, to produce anxiety. You are in a new building, surrounded by strangers who are more experienced than you, doing unfamiliar tasks, and being assessed at every turn. Anxiety in this context is completely normal and completely understandable. The problem is when it manifests in ways that undermine your performance.

Common anxiety behaviours that hurt your assessment:
- Over-talking in conversations because silence feels threatening
- Under-asking questions because you do not want to seem unintelligent
- Over-preparing for visible moments (case study, presentation) at the expense of the informal assessment (conversations, networking)
- Comparing yourself constantly to others in your cohort

The reframe that helps: everyone else in the room is also anxious. The analysts remember being anxious. The partners were anxious on their own spring weeks. What you are being assessed on is not the absence of nerves but your ability to function despite them.

### The reset protocol

If you have a bad moment (a blank in a conversation, a weak answer in a session, a presentation that did not go well), you need a quick reset protocol to prevent one bad moment from affecting the rest of the day.

The 10-minute reset: after a bad moment, find somewhere quiet (bathroom, stairwell, outside briefly). Take 5-10 slow breaths. Write down two things: what specifically did not go well, and what you will do differently in the next conversation. Then go back in. The self-awareness you are practising with this protocol is also a skill that banks value.

Do not dwell on bad moments in the middle of the week. Dwell on them at 11pm, before you go to sleep, when you have time to actually process and adjust.

### Energy management across the week

A spring week is a marathon, not a sprint. Managing your energy matters.

Sleep. Seriously. The temptation to stay up late every evening preparing for the next day is understandable but counterproductive. A well-rested person who has prepared adequately performs better than an exhausted person who has prepared exhaustively. Aim for 7 hours minimum.

Food and hydration. Obvious but regularly ignored. The buffet at the networking event is for eating, not just for standing next to while you network. Eat something. Stay hydrated. Long days in air-conditioned offices are dehydrating.

Downtime. If there is free time built into the programme (lunch break, early finish one day), use some of it to be genuinely offline. Not to prepare for the next day. Not to draft LinkedIn messages. Just to reset.

### The cohort dynamic

You will form impressions of the other spring weekers in your cohort within the first few hours. Some of those impressions will be accurate. Some will not. Be careful about dismissing people early. The person you thought was going to be annoying might turn out to be the most well-connected person in the room. The person you thought was brilliant might not perform well under pressure.

The cohort relationship is also being observed. How you treat people you are technically competing with says something about your character. Firms (especially consulting firms and smaller boutiques) will ask staff members: "Which spring weekers worked well with the rest of the group?" You want your name to come up in that conversation.

## A Final Note

Spring weeks are intense, often surreal, and occasionally unfair. You can do everything in this handbook and still not get the offer. Desk allocation, cohort dynamics, and timing all play a role in outcomes that are partly outside your control.

What you can control is your preparation, your attitude, and your follow-through. The students who read this handbook, implement it seriously, and treat every interaction as an opportunity to learn and impress are playing a fundamentally different game from the students who show up and hope for the best.

The return offer is not given to the most intelligent student in the room. It is given to the student who was consistently good, professionally composed, and genuinely interested in being there - all week, not just on the days that felt easy.

You know more than you think you do. Now go show them.
